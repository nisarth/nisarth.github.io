Link Building Strategies That Actually Work in 2026

April 10, 2026 · 14 min read · By Nisarth Patel

Contents

    Backlinks remain one of the most powerful ranking factors in Google's algorithm. That has not changed in 2026, and I do not see it changing any time soon. What has changed -- dramatically -- is how you need to earn them. The days of submitting your site to a hundred directories, spinning articles across guest post farms, or buying links from Fiverr sellers are not just ineffective now. They are actively dangerous to your rankings. Google's SpamBrain algorithm has become exceptionally good at identifying and devaluing manipulative link patterns, and manual actions for unnatural links can set your SEO back by months or even years.

    I have been building links for clients across different industries for over four years now -- from local businesses in Ahmedabad to SaaS companies targeting global markets, from e-commerce brands to professional service firms. Through that experience, I have developed a clear picture of what works, what wastes time, and what gets you into trouble. The strategies in this guide are the ones I actually use in my day-to-day work. Every tactic here has generated real, measurable results for real businesses. None of them involve shortcuts or schemes that put your site at risk.

    Before we get into the specific strategies, let me be clear about something: link building is hard work. There is no magic formula. The best links come from creating genuine value, building real relationships, and consistently putting in the effort. If someone promises you fifty high-authority backlinks in a week for a few hundred dollars, they are either lying or using tactics that will eventually harm your site. With that reality check out of the way, let us look at what actually works.

    Why Backlinks Still Matter in 2026

    Every year, someone publishes an article claiming that backlinks are dead. Every year, they are wrong. Google's own documentation continues to reference links as a key ranking signal, and every serious correlation study I have seen confirms that pages with more high-quality backlinks tend to rank higher. Ahrefs' latest study of over eleven million search results found a clear correlation between the number of referring domains and organic search traffic. The top-ranking result for any given keyword has an average of 3.8 times more backlinks than positions two through ten.

    What has changed is the weighting. Google's algorithm has become much better at evaluating link quality. A single link from a relevant, authoritative site in your niche now carries more weight than it did five years ago, while low-quality links carry less weight or are simply ignored. Google has also gotten better at understanding topical relevance -- a link from a site that covers related topics to yours is worth significantly more than a random link from an unrelated site, even if that unrelated site has a higher domain authority score.

    The introduction of AI-driven search features like AI Overviews has added another dimension. Google's AI systems use authority signals, including backlinks, to determine which sources to cite in generated responses. If you want your content to appear in AI-powered search results, having a strong backlink profile that demonstrates expertise and authority in your topic area is more important than ever. I have noticed that sites with strong on-page optimisation combined with quality backlinks are significantly more likely to be cited in AI Overviews than sites with good content but weak link profiles.

    The Evolution of Guest Posting

    Guest posting has been a link building staple for over a decade, and it still works in 2026 -- but the approach that succeeds today looks nothing like what worked in 2015. Back then, you could find hundreds of blogs accepting guest posts, churn out mediocre 500-word articles, stuff a link in the author bio, and see results. That approach is dead. Google has explicitly stated that large-scale guest posting campaigns for the primary purpose of link building violate their guidelines.

    The guest posting that works today is what I call strategic contribution. Instead of mass-outreaching to any blog that will accept your content, you identify a small number of high-quality publications that your target audience actually reads. You study their content, understand what topics they have not covered well, and pitch genuinely useful articles that fill gaps in their editorial calendar. The link you earn is almost secondary -- the primary value is the exposure to a relevant audience and the association with a trusted publication.

    Here is my process for effective guest posting in 2026. First, I build a prospect list of twenty to thirty publications in the client's niche using a combination of Google searches like "write for us" + [industry], Ahrefs' Content Explorer to find sites that publish frequently on relevant topics, and manual research of publications I know their audience reads. Second, I qualify each prospect by checking their domain authority, traffic levels in Ahrefs or Semrush, editorial standards, and whether they link to contributor websites with dofollow links. Third, I study their existing content to find angle gaps -- topics they should be covering but have not, or existing articles that could be improved with a fresh perspective. Fourth, I send personalised pitches that reference specific articles on their site and propose a unique angle with a brief outline.

    The response rate for cold guest post pitches is typically between five and fifteen percent when done well. I have found that including two or three specific topic ideas in the initial email, rather than asking what they need, significantly improves response rates. Editors are busy. Making their decision easy by providing concrete, ready-to-approve pitches is the fastest way to get a yes. For one SaaS client, this approach earned us guest posts on three industry-leading publications over six months, resulting in a thirty-seven percent increase in referring domains and a measurable improvement in keyword rankings for their primary commercial terms.

    Digital PR and Newsjacking

    Digital PR is the link building strategy with the highest ceiling. A single successful digital PR campaign can earn you links from dozens or even hundreds of authoritative news sites and industry publications -- the kind of links that would take years to build through other methods. The challenge is that it requires creativity, timing, and a genuine news angle.

    The core principle of digital PR for link building is simple: create something that journalists and bloggers want to write about and reference. This could be original research, a unique data set, an expert opinion on a trending topic, or a tool or resource that solves a common problem. The key is that it needs to be genuinely interesting or useful, not just a thinly veiled promotion of your business.

    Newsjacking is a specific digital PR tactic that involves inserting your expertise into breaking news stories. When a major development happens in your industry, journalists are actively looking for expert commentary. If you can provide an informed, quotable opinion quickly, you can earn links from major publications within hours. I monitor Google Alerts, Twitter trending topics, and industry news feeds daily for newsjacking opportunities. Speed is critical -- you need to be one of the first experts to respond. I keep a list of ready-to-go commentary templates for predictable industry events so I can respond within thirty minutes of a news story breaking.

    For a fintech client, we newsjacked a major regulatory announcement by publishing a detailed analysis within two hours of the news breaking. The analysis was picked up by four industry publications and two mainstream business news sites, earning six high-authority backlinks in a single week. That is the power of digital PR when you combine expertise with speed.

    Data-driven press releases are another effective digital PR approach. If your business generates any kind of data -- customer surveys, transaction data, usage statistics -- you can package that data into a story that journalists find compelling. The data needs to reveal something surprising, counter-intuitive, or trend-confirming. "Our sales increased twenty percent" is not a story. "Seventy-three percent of Indian small businesses have increased their digital marketing spend by over fifty percent since 2024" is a story that business journalists would want to cover.

    HARO, Connectively, and Journalist Request Platforms

    Help A Reporter Out (HARO) was rebranded to Connectively by Cision, and despite the name change, the concept remains one of the most reliable ways to earn high-authority backlinks. The platform connects journalists who need expert sources with professionals who can provide quotes and insights. When a journalist includes your quote in their article, they typically link back to your website.

    I have been using HARO and now Connectively for over three years, and it has earned links from publications I never could have reached through cold outreach alone. The trick is treating it as a daily practice rather than an occasional activity. I spend fifteen to twenty minutes each morning reviewing journalist requests and responding to any that match my expertise or my clients' areas of knowledge. Consistency matters far more than volume -- responding to three highly relevant queries with thoughtful, detailed answers is better than blasting fifty generic responses.

    Here are the rules I follow for successful Connectively pitches. First, respond within a few hours of the query being published. Journalists often work on tight deadlines, and late responses get ignored regardless of quality. Second, lead with your credentials. Start your response by briefly explaining why you are qualified to comment on this topic. Third, provide a specific, quotable answer. Journalists want ready-to-use quotes, not vague generalisations. Give them a complete thought in two to three sentences that they can drop directly into their article. Fourth, include one or two unique data points or examples if possible. Fifth, never pitch your services in the response. If your quote is good enough, the journalist will link to you. Being overly promotional gets your response deleted immediately.

    Beyond Connectively, platforms like Quoted, SourceBottle, and Terkel operate on similar models. I recommend monitoring at least two of these platforms simultaneously to maximise your exposure to journalist requests. For one client in the health and wellness space, consistent Connectively responses over eight months earned us links from publications including Healthline, MindBodyGreen, and three regional news outlets. Those links alone were worth more than any guest posting campaign could have delivered in the same timeframe.

    Broken Link Building

    Broken link building is one of the most underrated strategies in my toolkit. The concept is straightforward: you find broken links on other websites, create content that replaces the dead resource, and reach out to the site owner to suggest your content as a replacement. It works because you are solving a problem for the website owner rather than just asking for a favour.

    Here is my step-by-step process. I start by identifying relevant resource pages, industry blogs, and publications in the client's niche using Ahrefs' Content Explorer. Then I run those pages through Ahrefs' Broken Link Checker or the Check My Links Chrome extension to find outbound links that return 404 errors. For each broken link, I check what the dead page used to contain using the Wayback Machine on archive.org. If the dead content is something my client already covers or could reasonably create, I have a prospect.

    The outreach email is critical. I always lead with the broken link I found -- this immediately establishes that I am providing value, not just asking for something. I mention the specific page where the broken link exists, the anchor text and URL of the dead link, and offer my client's content as a replacement. I frame it as a suggestion to help them maintain their page quality, not as a request for a link. Here is a rough template I use:

    "Hi [Name], I was reading your excellent resource on [topic] and noticed that the link to [dead resource] in your [section/paragraph] appears to be broken -- it returns a 404 error. I recently published a comprehensive guide on the same topic that might work as a replacement: [URL]. Either way, thought you would want to know about the broken link. Cheers, Nisarth."

    The response rate for broken link building outreach typically runs between five and twelve percent in my experience, which is comparable to guest post pitching. The advantage is that each successful outreach earns you a contextual, in-content link rather than an author bio link, which carries more SEO value. For a B2B client, I found forty-seven broken links across resource pages in their industry over two months. Twelve of those led to link placements, contributing to a significant improvement in rankings for their core service pages.

    Resource Page Link Building

    Resource pages are curated lists of links that a website publishes to help their audience find useful information on a specific topic. They exist in almost every industry -- universities maintain resource pages for students, government sites link to useful tools, industry associations list helpful guides, and bloggers create "best of" roundup pages. Getting your content included on these pages earns you highly relevant, contextual backlinks.

    Finding resource pages is straightforward. I use Google search operators like "useful resources" + [industry keyword], "helpful links" + [topic], inurl:resources + [keyword], and intitle:"useful links" + [niche]. I also check university sites specifically because .edu links carry strong authority signals. For a client in the education technology space, we earned three .edu links by getting their free tools included on university resource pages -- links that would have been nearly impossible to acquire through any other method.

    The key to resource page outreach is having genuinely useful content to offer. Resource page curators are looking for comprehensive, well-produced content that adds value to their existing list. A thin 500-word blog post will not cut it. You need in-depth guides, tools, calculators, or data-driven resources that clearly belong alongside the other links on the page. Before reaching out, I always review the existing links on the resource page to ensure my client's content matches the quality standard and is not duplicating something already listed.

    The outreach approach is similar to broken link building -- be helpful, be specific, and be brief. Mention the specific resource page, explain why your content would be a valuable addition for their audience, and include a one-sentence description of what the resource covers. Do not ask them to add your link. Instead, suggest that it might be useful for their readers and let them make the decision. This soft approach consistently outperforms hard asks in my experience.

    Creating Linkable Assets

    The most sustainable link building strategy is creating content so valuable that people link to it naturally, without you having to ask. These are called linkable assets, and they form the foundation of every long-term link building campaign I run. A linkable asset is any piece of content that provides unique value that cannot be easily found elsewhere.

    The types of linkable assets that consistently earn links include original research and surveys. If you can produce data that does not exist elsewhere, journalists and bloggers will reference it. I helped a client in the recruitment industry conduct an annual salary survey of over two thousand professionals in their sector. That single piece of content has earned over sixty backlinks over two years because it is the only comprehensive salary data available for that specific niche. Every year when we update it, it earns a fresh wave of links.

    Free tools and calculators are another powerful linkable asset category. Interactive tools that solve a specific problem earn links continuously because people reference them when recommending solutions. A mortgage calculator, an SEO audit tool, a tax estimation calculator -- these are the kinds of resources that accumulate backlinks organically over time. The development cost can be significant, but the ROI in terms of sustained link acquisition makes it worthwhile for businesses that can invest upfront.

    Comprehensive guides and definitive resources work when they are genuinely the best resource available on a topic. Not just a good article -- the definitive, go-to reference that other content creators link to because they cannot justify covering the topic in equal depth themselves. This is where a strong keyword research strategy helps you identify topics where no truly comprehensive resource exists yet. The article you are reading right now is an example of this approach -- I aim to make it the most practical, experience-driven guide on link building available, so that other SEO professionals reference it when discussing the topic.

    Infographics and visual data have become less effective than they were five years ago, but well-designed visualisations of complex data still earn links, particularly when the underlying data is original. The key is that the visual must communicate something that is genuinely easier to understand as an image than as text. A pie chart of publicly available data adds nothing. An original visualisation that maps the relationship between multiple data points in a way that reveals a pattern -- that earns links.

    Podcast Guesting as a Link Building Channel

    Podcast guesting has become one of my favourite link building tactics over the past two years, and I think it is still underutilised by most SEOs. Here is why it works: when you appear as a guest on a podcast, the host almost always links to your website in the show notes. These links are typically dofollow, contextual, and come from established sites with real audiences. Beyond the link itself, you get brand exposure, the opportunity to demonstrate expertise, and referral traffic from listeners who look you up after the episode.

    Finding podcast opportunities is easier than you might think. I use platforms like Podchaser and ListenNotes to search for podcasts in my clients' industries. I also look at where competitors have appeared as guests by checking their backlink profiles in Ahrefs and filtering for podcast-related domains. If a competitor has been on a particular podcast, there is a good chance that podcast would be interested in your perspective as well, especially if you can offer a different angle.

    The pitch to podcast hosts follows similar principles to guest post pitching. Be specific about what value you can bring to their audience. Propose two or three specific episode topics rather than a vague offer to "share your expertise." Reference a recent episode you enjoyed and explain how your topic would complement their existing content. Podcast hosts receive far fewer guest pitches than blog editors, so your response rate will typically be higher -- I see twenty to thirty percent response rates for well-crafted podcast pitches, compared to five to fifteen percent for guest post pitches.

    One tactical tip: after the episode airs, create a companion blog post on your own site that expands on the topics you discussed. Link to the podcast episode and share it on social media, tagging the host. This builds the relationship, increases the likelihood of being invited back, and creates additional content that can rank for related keywords. For my own work, podcast appearances have been responsible for some of the strongest backlinks in my profile, including links from sites with domain ratings above seventy that I never could have earned through cold outreach alone.

    Competitor Backlink Analysis

    One of the most efficient ways to find link building opportunities is to study where your competitors are getting their links. If a site has linked to your competitor, there is a reasonable chance they would link to you too, especially if your content is equal to or better than what they are currently linking to.

    I use Ahrefs for competitor backlink analysis because their link database is the largest and most frequently updated. Here is my process. First, I identify three to five direct competitors who rank for the keywords I am targeting. Second, I pull their complete backlink profiles and export them. Third, I use the Link Intersect tool to find sites that link to two or more of my competitors but not to my client -- these are the highest-probability prospects because they have already demonstrated a willingness to link to similar content. Fourth, I categorise the opportunities by type: guest posts, resource pages, editorial mentions, directory listings, and so on. Fifth, I prioritise by domain authority and relevance, then create a tailored outreach plan for each category.

    The Link Intersect approach is particularly powerful. When I find a site that links to three of my client's competitors, the probability of earning a link from that site is substantially higher than from a random prospect. These sites are clearly interested in the topic, they link externally, and they are familiar with businesses similar to my client. The outreach message almost writes itself: "I noticed you link to [competitor A] and [competitor B] on your [page]. I have published a resource on the same topic that includes [unique angle or additional information]. It might be a useful addition for your readers."

    Beyond direct competitor analysis, I also analyse the backlink profiles of top-ranking content for my target keywords. Sometimes the best link opportunities come from sites that link to content ranking for the same keywords but are not direct business competitors. A non-competing blog that links to a general guide on your topic is an excellent prospect for resource page outreach or broken link building if any of their current links are dead.

    Data-Driven Content That Earns Links

    I mentioned original research earlier as a linkable asset, but it deserves its own section because data-driven content is the single most effective type of content for earning backlinks at scale. Journalists, bloggers, and content creators all need data to support their arguments. If your data is the primary source, they have to cite you.

    There are several ways to generate original data even if you are not a research firm. Customer surveys are the most accessible option. A well-designed survey of one hundred to five hundred respondents in your industry can produce data points that get cited for years. I use Google Forms or Typeform for data collection and focus on questions that will generate surprising or counter-intuitive findings -- those are the statistics that get quoted and linked to. A finding like "sixty-two percent of small business owners spend less than two hours per week on SEO despite ranking it as their top marketing priority" is inherently interesting and quotable.

    Internal data analysis is another approach. If your business processes transactions, user interactions, or any kind of measurable activity, you can anonymise and aggregate that data into industry insights. An e-commerce platform might publish data on average order values by category and season. A project management tool might share data on how remote teams distribute their working hours. The data is already sitting in your database -- you just need to extract, anonymise, and package it compellingly.

    Public data aggregation and analysis works when original data is not available. Government databases, academic research, industry reports, and open data sets are all potential sources. The value you add is in the analysis, visualisation, and interpretation. Taking raw data from multiple sources, combining it, and drawing meaningful conclusions creates something new and linkable even though the underlying data is public. I did this for a real estate client by aggregating property price data from government records with demographic data from census reports to create neighbourhood investment guides. The guides earned links from property portals, financial blogs, and local news outlets because no one else had combined and analysed those data sources in that way.

    When you publish data-driven content, make the key statistics easy to quote by formatting them as standalone callout blocks or bullet points. Include your brand name alongside the data point so that anyone quoting the statistic naturally mentions your business. And always include a methodology section that explains how the data was collected and analysed -- this builds trust and makes journalists more comfortable citing your numbers.

    Avoiding Link Schemes and Toxic Practices

    I cannot write a guide on link building without addressing the practices you need to avoid. Google's guidelines are clear about what constitutes a link scheme, and the penalties for violating them are severe. Here are the tactics that I see businesses still using in 2026 that I strongly advise against.

    Buying links is the most obvious violation. This includes paying for guest posts where the primary purpose is the link, purchasing links from link brokers, and paying for sponsored posts without using rel="sponsored" tags. Google's SpamBrain algorithm has become remarkably effective at detecting paid link patterns, even when the links come from seemingly legitimate sites. I have seen businesses lose fifty percent or more of their organic traffic after a manual action for unnatural links. The recovery process takes months and there is no guarantee you will fully recover. It is simply not worth the risk.

    Private Blog Networks (PBNs) involve creating or purchasing a network of websites solely for the purpose of linking to your main site. PBNs were effective years ago, but Google has invested heavily in detecting them. Common signals include shared hosting, similar site structures, thin content, and unnatural interlinking patterns. When Google identifies a PBN, they devalue every link from the network simultaneously, which can cause sudden and dramatic ranking drops. I have been contacted by multiple businesses over the years who built their entire SEO strategy on PBN links and lost everything overnight when Google caught on.

    Link exchanges and reciprocal linking schemes involve agreements like "I will link to you if you link to me." Occasional natural reciprocal links between related businesses are fine and expected. What Google penalises is systematic, large-scale link exchange programmes. If twenty percent or more of your backlinks are reciprocal, that is a red flag. I once audited a site that had over three hundred reciprocal links from a link exchange programme. After removing those links and submitting a disavow file, their rankings actually improved because Google was previously suppressing the site due to the unnatural pattern.

    Automated link building tools and services that create links through blog comment spam, forum signatures, wiki edits, or web 2.0 profile pages generate the kind of low-quality, irrelevant links that Google either ignores entirely or penalises. These links waste your time and money and add nothing to your SEO. If someone offers you thousands of backlinks for a low price, this is what they are selling.

    The safest rule is simple: if you would not want a Google employee to look at a link and understand why it exists, do not build it. Every link in your profile should have a clear, legitimate reason for existing beyond SEO.

    Measuring Link Building Success

    Link building is an investment, and like any investment, you need to measure its returns. Here are the metrics I track for every link building campaign.

    Referring domains growth is the primary metric. Not total backlinks, but unique referring domains. One hundred links from one site count as one referring domain. Ahrefs and Semrush both track this over time. I look at month-over-month referring domain growth as the key indicator of whether our link building efforts are producing results. For most small to medium businesses, adding five to fifteen new quality referring domains per month is a strong pace. Enterprise campaigns might target thirty to fifty or more.

    Domain Rating and Domain Authority trends are secondary metrics. These are proprietary scores from Ahrefs and Moz respectively, not Google metrics. They correlate with ranking ability but are not perfect predictors. I track them as directional indicators rather than absolute targets. If your domain rating is steadily climbing while you are building links, that is a good sign. If it is flat or declining, something is off.

    Keyword ranking improvements are the ultimate measure of link building effectiveness. I track rankings for our target keywords weekly and look for upward trends that correlate with link acquisition. It is important to note that rankings are affected by many factors beyond links, so I never attribute ranking changes solely to link building. But if we earn ten new quality backlinks to a page and see it move from position fifteen to position seven over the following three months, the link building likely contributed. I also monitor which specific pages receive the most link equity and whether that aligns with our priority pages. Sometimes links land on blog content when we actually need authority flowing to commercial pages. In those cases, I strengthen internal linking structures to distribute the link equity where it is needed most.

    Referral traffic from backlinks is a metric that many SEOs overlook but I consider essential. A good backlink does not just pass PageRank -- it sends actual visitors to your site. If a link from a high-authority site is generating zero referral traffic, it might be placed in a location where no one sees it, or on a page that itself receives no traffic. Referral traffic also serves as a quality signal: links that drive real, engaged visitors are the kind of links Google values most.

    Building a Sustainable Link Building Strategy

    The biggest mistake I see businesses make with link building is treating it as a one-time campaign rather than an ongoing practice. You do a burst of link building for three months, see some results, then stop. Over the following year, competitors continue building links while your profile stagnates, and your rankings gradually decline. Link building needs to be a consistent, sustained effort.

    Here is how I structure a sustainable link building strategy for clients. Each month, I allocate effort across multiple tactics rather than relying on a single approach. A typical monthly plan might include: responding to fifteen to twenty Connectively queries, conducting outreach for two to three guest post opportunities, running broken link building analysis on ten to fifteen resource pages in the client's niche, publishing one piece of data-driven or linkable asset content, pitching two to three podcast appearances, and monitoring competitor backlink profiles for new opportunities. This diversified approach ensures a steady flow of links from different sources, which creates a natural-looking backlink profile.

    I also build link building into the content strategy from the start. When planning content, I always ask: "Is this something people will want to link to?" Not every piece of content needs to be a linkable asset, but at least one piece per month should be designed specifically to attract links. This means investing in original research, comprehensive guides, free tools, or expert roundups that provide unique value your competitors cannot easily replicate.

    For startups and new businesses, I recommend starting with the lower-hanging fruit -- Connectively responses, broken link building, and podcast guesting -- while simultaneously investing in long-term linkable assets. The quick wins keep the momentum going while the bigger assets are in production. For established businesses with larger budgets, digital PR campaigns and original research typically deliver the highest ROI because the link volumes are larger and the links tend to come from higher-authority sources.

    Whatever your approach, remember that the best link building strategy is one you can sustain. Ten quality links per month for twelve months will always outperform a hundred links in one month followed by nothing. Consistency compounds, and in SEO, compounding is everything.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    How many backlinks do I need to rank on the first page of Google?

    There is no fixed number of backlinks required to rank on page one because it depends entirely on your niche, keyword difficulty, and the authority of competing pages. I have seen pages rank with fewer than ten high-quality backlinks for low-competition keywords, while competitive commercial terms might require hundreds. Focus on the quality and relevance of each link rather than chasing a specific count. A single link from an authoritative, topically relevant site can outweigh dozens of links from low-quality directories. Use Ahrefs' Keyword Difficulty metric to estimate the number of referring domains you will likely need for specific keywords.

    Is guest posting still effective for link building in 2026?

    Yes, guest posting remains effective when done correctly. The key difference between guest posting that works and guest posting that wastes your time is relevance and quality. Writing genuinely useful content for reputable publications in your niche builds real authority. Mass-submitting generic articles to low-quality blogs that accept anything does not. Google's spam detection has improved significantly, so the bar for what constitutes a valuable guest post is higher than ever. Focus on publications your target audience actually reads, and write content that would be worth reading even without the link.

    How long does it take to see results from link building?

    Link building is a long-term investment. From my experience, you typically start seeing measurable ranking improvements three to six months after acquiring quality backlinks, assuming your on-page SEO and content quality are solid. Some competitive niches take even longer. The initial links you build establish a foundation, and the compounding effect becomes more visible over time. I recommend tracking referring domain growth monthly and correlating it with ranking changes quarterly rather than expecting immediate results from individual links. Patience and consistency are essential.

    What is the difference between dofollow and nofollow links?

    Dofollow links pass PageRank and directly influence your search rankings. Nofollow links include a rel="nofollow" attribute that tells search engines not to pass ranking credit. However, since 2019, Google treats nofollow as a hint rather than a directive, meaning they may still consider nofollow links for ranking purposes. In practice, a natural backlink profile includes both types. A nofollow link from a high-traffic site like Reddit or a major news outlet still drives referral traffic and brand awareness, making it valuable even without direct SEO impact.

    Can link building get my site penalised by Google?

    Yes, manipulative link building can result in a manual action from Google, which can devastate your rankings. Practices that carry significant risk include buying links, participating in link exchange schemes, using private blog networks, and automated link building with software tools. Google's SpamBrain algorithm has become remarkably effective at detecting unnatural link patterns. The strategies I recommend in this article focus on earning links through genuine value creation, which carries no penalty risk. If you have acquired toxic links in the past, use Google's Disavow Tool to distance yourself from them. When in doubt, reach out for a backlink audit.

    💡
    The bottom line: Link building in 2026 is about earning links through genuine value, not manufacturing them through schemes. The strategies that work -- guest posting, digital PR, broken link building, resource page outreach, podcast guesting, and creating linkable assets -- all share a common thread: they provide real value to real people. That is why they earn real links. If you focus on consistency, quality, and relevance, your backlink profile will grow steadily, and your rankings will follow.

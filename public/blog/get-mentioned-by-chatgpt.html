How to Get Your Business Mentioned by ChatGPT and Perplexity

April 10, 2026 · 14 min read · By Nisarth Patel

Contents

    Something fundamental has changed in how people discover businesses online. Instead of typing a query into Google and scanning ten blue links, a growing number of users are asking ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, or Copilot questions like "What is the best SEO consultant in Ahmedabad?" or "Which project management tool should a startup use?" and getting direct answers with specific business recommendations. If your business is not showing up in these AI-generated responses, you are invisible to a rapidly growing segment of your potential audience. This guide is about fixing that.

    I have been working on Generative Engine Optimisation (GEO) strategies for my own brand and for clients since early 2025, and I have tracked which actions actually move the needle on AI mentions versus which ones are just theoretical noise. What follows is a practitioner's guide -- not a speculative thought piece -- built from real experiments, real results, and real failures across multiple industries and AI platforms.

    The stakes are significant. According to data from multiple analytics platforms, AI-assisted search is growing at roughly 40 percent year-over-year. Gartner predicted that by 2026, traditional search engine volume would drop by 25 percent as users shift to AI interfaces. Whether that exact number holds or not, the direction is clear. Businesses that figure out how to get recommended by AI tools now will have a compounding advantage over those that wait.

    How LLMs Actually Source Information

    Before you can optimise for AI mentions, you need to understand how these systems actually find and select the information they present. There are two fundamentally different mechanisms at play, and confusing them leads to wasted effort.

    Training data knowledge. Models like ChatGPT (GPT-4 and its successors) have a knowledge cutoff -- a point in time after which they have not seen new information. Everything the model "knows" was absorbed during training from a massive corpus of web pages, books, articles, code repositories, and other text sources. When ChatGPT recommends a business without searching the web, it is drawing from this training data. This means your historical web presence matters enormously. If your brand was well-represented across authoritative sources before the training cutoff, the model is more likely to mention you. If your business launched after the cutoff, or had minimal web presence before it, you are essentially invisible to the model's internal knowledge.

    Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). Perplexity, Bing Copilot, and Google AI Overviews use a different approach. They perform real-time web searches, retrieve relevant documents, and then use an LLM to synthesise an answer from those sources. This is why Perplexity shows citations -- it is literally telling you which web pages it pulled information from. For RAG-based systems, traditional SEO signals still matter because the retrieval step uses search engine results. If your pages rank well in conventional search, they are more likely to be retrieved and cited by these AI tools.

    The hybrid approach. Most AI tools now use some combination of both. ChatGPT has web browsing capabilities. Gemini integrates with Google Search. Even tools that primarily rely on training data are increasingly supplementing with real-time retrieval. This means you need to optimise for both mechanisms: build long-term entity authority that gets embedded in training data, and maintain strong SEO performance for real-time retrieval.

    Understanding this distinction has been critical for my strategy work. A client of mine -- a SaaS company in the HR tech space -- was frustrated that ChatGPT never mentioned them despite having a solid product. The reason was straightforward: they launched in 2024, had limited press coverage, and their domain authority was modest. ChatGPT's training data simply did not include enough information about them. We focused on Perplexity and Google AI Overviews first (where RAG gave us a faster path) while simultaneously building the entity authority needed for future ChatGPT training cycles.

    Building Entity Authority for AI Recognition

    Entity authority is the concept that determines whether an AI model considers your business a legitimate, trustworthy source worth mentioning. It is not a single metric -- it is a constellation of signals that collectively tell AI systems "this business is real, established, and authoritative in its domain."

    Consistent NAP across the web. Your business name, address, and phone number (NAP) need to be identical everywhere they appear online. I mean character-for-character identical. If your website says "Nisarth Patel Digital" but your LinkedIn says "Nisarth Patel - Digital Growth" and your Google Business Profile says "NP Digital Growth Specialist," AI models may treat these as three separate entities rather than one. I audit every client's brand mentions across at least twenty platforms and standardise them. This sounds tedious, and it is, but it is foundational.

    Authoritative third-party mentions. AI models weight information more heavily when it appears on authoritative, independent sources. A mention of your business in Forbes, TechCrunch, or a respected industry publication carries far more weight than a mention on your own blog. But you do not need tier-one press to build authority. Industry-specific publications, local news outlets, university websites, government directories, and well-established niche blogs all contribute. I have seen businesses gain AI mentions after being featured in just three or four mid-tier publications, as long as those publications were genuinely relevant to their industry.

    Professional profiles and directories. Complete, detailed profiles on LinkedIn, Crunchbase, AngelList, industry-specific directories, and professional associations all contribute to entity authority. Each one is an independent source confirming your business exists and operates in a specific domain. For local businesses, Google Business Profile is particularly important because Google AI Overviews pull heavily from it.

    Social proof signals. Reviews on Google, G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, and industry-specific review platforms add another layer of entity validation. AI models are trained on this data and use it to assess business quality and relevance. A business with 200 genuine reviews across multiple platforms is far more likely to be recommended than one with zero reviews, even if the zero-review business has a better product.

    Creating Citation-Worthy Content

    Not all content gets cited by AI tools. In my experience tracking which pages earn AI citations, there are clear patterns in what works and what gets ignored.

    Original data and research. Content that contains original statistics, survey results, case studies with specific numbers, or unique analyses gets cited disproportionately more than generic overview content. When I published an article with original data about SEO trends in the Indian market -- based on auditing 50 client websites -- it earned citations from both Perplexity and Google AI Overviews within weeks. The data was unique to my work, which meant no other source could provide it. AI tools had to cite me or not include that information at all.

    Definitive answers to specific questions. AI tools are fundamentally answer engines. They are looking for content that definitively answers questions. Content that hedges excessively, uses vague language, or buries the answer under layers of preamble gets passed over. I structure my content to lead with the answer and then provide supporting detail. A section titled "How much does SEO cost in India?" should start with a specific range ("Typical SEO retainers in India range from 15,000 to 1,50,000 INR per month depending on scope and agency experience") before explaining the variables. That opening sentence is exactly what an AI tool wants to extract and cite.

    Named frameworks and methodologies. If you create a named process or framework, AI tools are more likely to attribute it to you. I call my GEO audit approach the "Entity-Content-Signal" framework, and I reference it consistently across my content. Over time, this creates a pattern in the training data that associates that specific framework with my name. This is not just branding -- it is a deliberate strategy for AI attribution.

    Comparison and recommendation content. Articles that compare tools, services, or approaches are heavily cited by AI because users frequently ask comparison questions. "Best project management tools for remote teams" or "Ahrefs vs Semrush for technical SEO" -- these queries are answered by AI tools pulling from comparison content. If your business creates thorough, honest comparison content in your niche, you become a source that AI tools rely on for recommendation queries.

    Content structure matters enormously. Use clear H2 and H3 headings that mirror the questions people ask. Include a concise summary or key takeaway at the beginning of each section. Use lists and tables for comparative information. Include your brand name naturally within the content -- not stuffed in, but mentioned in context where it logically belongs. These structural choices make your content easier for AI models to parse, extract, and cite. I cover content structure for AI in more detail in my complete GEO strategy guide.

    Wikipedia and Wikidata: The Entity Backbone

    Wikipedia and Wikidata occupy a unique position in the AI ecosystem. They are primary sources for entity information in virtually every major LLM's training data. Having a Wikipedia article about your business or being listed as an entity in Wikidata is one of the strongest signals of entity legitimacy you can have.

    The Wikipedia challenge. Let me be direct: getting a Wikipedia article for your business is difficult and should not be your first priority. Wikipedia has strict notability guidelines that require significant coverage in independent, reliable sources. Most small and medium businesses do not meet these criteria. Attempting to create a Wikipedia article that does not meet notability standards will result in deletion and potentially a blacklist. I have seen businesses hire Wikipedia editors to create articles, only to have them deleted within hours. This wastes time and money.

    When Wikipedia is realistic. If your business has been featured in multiple major publications, has won industry awards, has a unique story that has attracted press coverage, or operates at a scale that naturally generates news coverage, a Wikipedia article may be viable. The key is that the sources must already exist before you attempt to create the article. Wikipedia editors will verify every claim against independent sources.

    Wikidata is more accessible. Wikidata is the structured data backbone of Wikipedia, and its notability requirements are less stringent. You can create a Wikidata item for your business that includes structured properties like founding date, location, industry, key people, and website. AI models query Wikidata extensively for entity information. Creating a well-structured Wikidata entry with proper claims and references is something most established businesses can do, and it meaningfully improves your entity recognition by AI systems.

    Practical Wikidata steps. Create an account on Wikidata, then create a new item for your business. Add statements including "instance of" (set to "business" or a more specific type), "country," "official website," "founded by," and "inception date." Add references for each claim -- these should link to verifiable sources like your official website, LinkedIn profiles, or press mentions. I typically spend about two hours setting up a comprehensive Wikidata entry for a client, and the entity recognition benefits begin appearing in AI responses within weeks as models refresh their knowledge graphs.

    Structured Data as an AI Signal

    Structured data has always been important for SEO, but it takes on additional significance for AI optimisation. AI models and the retrieval systems that feed them use structured data to understand entities, relationships, and factual claims on your pages.

    Organisation schema. Implement comprehensive Organisation (or LocalBusiness) schema on your homepage that includes your business name, description, founding date, founders, address, contact information, social media profiles, and logo. This creates a machine-readable entity card that AI systems can parse directly. I include the sameAs property linking to every official profile -- LinkedIn, Twitter, Crunchbase, Google Business Profile, Wikidata -- to explicitly connect all representations of your entity.

    Person schema for key people. If thought leadership and personal branding are part of your strategy (and they should be for most service businesses), implement Person schema for key team members. Include name, job title, affiliation, social profiles, and links to authored content. When an AI tool encounters a question like "Who are the best SEO consultants in India?", it is connecting person entities to expertise signals. If my Person schema clearly states my role, expertise area, and links to my published content, I am more easily identified as a relevant entity for that query.

    Article and FAQ schema. Every content piece should have Article schema with proper author attribution, publication date, and description. FAQ schema is particularly powerful for AI citation because it structures question-answer pairs in exactly the format that AI tools need. When Perplexity retrieves your page and finds a clean FAQ section with schema markup, it can confidently extract and cite specific answers. I implement FAQPage schema on every significant content page I publish.

    Speakable schema. This is a newer schema type that tells AI and voice assistants which sections of your content are most suitable for direct quotation. I mark the introduction and key takeaway sections of every article as speakable. It is a forward-looking implementation that positions your content well as AI tools become more sophisticated about selecting quotable passages.

    Building Brand Mentions Across the Web

    Brand mentions -- even without backlinks -- are a significant signal for AI entity recognition. AI models learn about entities not just from your own website, but from every mention of your brand across the web. The more contexts in which your brand appears alongside relevant topic signals, the stronger your entity association becomes.

    Digital PR and thought leadership. Contribute guest articles to industry publications. Participate in podcast interviews. Speak at conferences and webinars. Each of these generates brand mentions in contexts that AI models consider authoritative. I track brand mentions using tools like Brand24 and Google Alerts, and I aim for at least four new authoritative mentions per month. The mentions do not need to include links -- AI models process text, not just link graphs.

    Forum and community participation. Active participation in relevant communities -- Reddit, Quora, Stack Exchange, industry-specific forums -- generates brand mentions in discussion contexts. AI models are heavily trained on forum data because it contains authentic, experience-based information. When I answer questions on Quora about SEO strategies and naturally reference my experience or methodologies, those mentions contribute to my entity recognition. The key word is "naturally" -- blatant self-promotion gets downvoted and removed, which is counterproductive.

    Social media consistency. Maintain active, consistent profiles on LinkedIn, Twitter, and platforms relevant to your industry. Post regularly about your area of expertise. AI models are trained on social media data, and a consistent, active social presence reinforces your entity's topical associations. I publish at least three LinkedIn posts per week about SEO, GEO, and digital growth. Each post is another data point associating my name with these topics in future training data.

    Citation building on authoritative directories. List your business on every relevant industry directory, professional association, and business listing platform. Crunchbase, Clutch, G2, local chamber of commerce directories, industry association member lists -- each one is an independent confirmation of your entity's existence and domain. For a client in the legal tech space, we built listings on 35 directories over two months, and their entity recognition by AI tools measurably improved.

    Content Freshness and Update Signals

    AI tools, particularly RAG-based systems like Perplexity, prefer fresh content. Outdated content with stale statistics, references to deprecated tools, or advice that no longer applies gets deprioritised in favour of recently updated material.

    Regular content updates. I update my most important articles at least quarterly. Not superficial changes -- genuine updates that add new information, refresh statistics, incorporate recent developments, and expand sections based on new experience. Each update gets a new dateModified value in the Article schema and visible "Last updated" text. This signals freshness to both search engines and AI retrieval systems.

    Evergreen content with current examples. The best strategy is to create fundamentally evergreen content that you regularly refresh with current examples and data. My technical SEO audit checklist is structured as a timeless process, but I update the specific tools, thresholds, and platform references annually. This means the content remains relevant for AI citation year-round while the freshness signals keep it competitive in retrieval.

    Publishing cadence. A consistent publishing schedule signals to AI systems that your site is actively maintained and producing current information. I publish at least two new articles per month and update four existing ones. The combination of new content and updated content creates a persistent freshness signal across my entire site. For clients, I recommend a minimum of one new article and two updates per month -- more if resources allow.

    News hooks and timely content. When significant developments happen in your industry -- algorithm updates, new regulations, tool launches, market shifts -- publishing timely analysis positions you as a current, relevant source. Perplexity is particularly responsive to timely content because its retrieval favours recent pages. I keep a list of topics I can write about quickly when relevant news breaks, with templates partially prepared so I can publish within hours rather than days.

    Monitoring Your AI Mentions

    You cannot improve what you do not measure. Monitoring how and when AI tools mention your business is essential for refining your strategy.

    Manual testing protocol. I maintain a list of 30 to 50 queries relevant to my business and test them across ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Copilot monthly. I record whether my business or content is mentioned, what context it appears in, what sources are cited, and what competitors are mentioned instead. This manual process takes about two hours per month, but the insights are invaluable. I have discovered entire topic areas where competitors were being recommended that I had not even considered creating content for.

    Perplexity citations tracking. Perplexity is the easiest AI tool to track because it shows its sources explicitly. I check Perplexity for my target queries weekly and note which of my pages are being cited, how they are being described, and what competing sources appear alongside them. When I notice a competitor's page being cited for a query I should own, I analyse their content to understand why it was selected over mine.

    Tools for AI mention monitoring. Several tools have emerged specifically for tracking AI mentions. Otterly.ai monitors how your brand appears across multiple AI platforms and tracks changes over time. Profound.ai provides similar monitoring with competitive benchmarking. These tools are still maturing, but they save significant time over purely manual monitoring. I use Otterly.ai for ongoing tracking and supplement with manual spot-checks.

    Google Search Console for AI Overviews. Google Search Console now provides data on impressions and clicks from AI Overviews. Check the Search Results appearance filter for AI Overview mentions. This data tells you which queries trigger AI Overviews that reference your content, how many impressions those generate, and what your click-through rate is from AI-generated responses versus standard results.

    The Practical Step-by-Step Strategy

    Here is the exact sequence I follow when a client asks me to get their business mentioned by AI tools. This is not theoretical -- it is the process I have refined over dozens of engagements.

    Week 1-2: Entity audit. I start by searching for the business across ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Copilot using ten to twenty relevant queries. I document what currently appears, identify gaps, and note competitors who are being mentioned. I then audit the business's entire web presence: website, social profiles, directories, press mentions, reviews, and structured data. This gives me a baseline and a clear picture of where the entity signals are weak.

    Week 3-4: Foundation fixes. I standardise the business name, description, and key details across every platform. I implement or fix Organisation, Person, and Article schema. I create or update the Wikidata entry. I ensure the llms.txt file is in place and properly structured. I set up proper AI crawler management in robots.txt. These foundational elements need to be solid before any content strategy will work effectively.

    Month 2-3: Content creation. I identify the top twenty queries where the client should appear in AI responses but currently does not. For each query, I create or substantially update a content piece that provides the definitive answer. Each piece follows citation-worthy content principles: original insights, specific data, named frameworks, clear structure, proper schema markup, and FAQ sections. I prioritise queries where RAG-based tools (Perplexity, Google AI Overviews) can pick up the content quickly rather than waiting for training data cycles.

    Month 3-4: Authority building. With the content foundation in place, I focus on building third-party brand mentions. This involves guest posting on industry publications, participating in podcasts, contributing expert quotes to journalists (using platforms like HARO or Qwoted), building directory listings, and encouraging customer reviews on relevant platforms. Each mention reinforces the entity signals and increases the likelihood of AI citation.

    Month 4-6: Monitoring and iteration. I run the full monitoring protocol monthly, tracking progress across all AI platforms. Where mentions are appearing, I double down on similar content. Where they are not, I analyse why -- is it a content quality issue, an authority gap, or a structural problem? I update underperforming content, create new pieces to fill gaps, and continue building brand mentions. This iterative phase is ongoing, but by month six, most clients see measurable progress in AI visibility.

    The compound effect. The beautiful thing about this strategy is that it compounds. Every piece of citation-worthy content, every brand mention, every structured data implementation, and every directory listing builds on everything that came before. After six months of consistent effort, the velocity of AI mentions typically accelerates because the entity authority has crossed a threshold where AI models confidently recommend the business. I have seen clients go from zero AI mentions to being the first recommendation for their primary queries within eight months using this exact process.

    Common Mistakes That Prevent AI Mentions

    Over the past eighteen months of GEO work, I have identified several recurring mistakes that businesses make when trying to get mentioned by AI tools.

    Focusing only on your own website. Your website is one piece of the puzzle, but AI entity recognition depends on a broad web presence. I have seen businesses with excellent on-site content that never get AI mentions because they have zero third-party brand mentions, no review presence, and incomplete directory listings. Your entity needs to be corroborated across multiple independent sources.

    Inconsistent brand information. As I mentioned earlier, inconsistency in your brand name, description, location, and key details across platforms confuses AI models. I recently audited a company that used four different variations of their business name across their web presence. The AI models were treating them as separate entities, diluting the authority of each one. Standardisation is boring but essential.

    Creating sales copy instead of informational content. AI tools skip over content that reads like a sales pitch. They are looking for informational, expert-level content that answers user questions. If every page on your site is a thinly-disguised sales page, AI models will not cite you because they do not consider your content helpful for answering questions. Create genuinely useful informational content alongside your commercial pages.

    Neglecting structured data. Many businesses implement basic schema markup or skip it entirely. In a world where AI systems parse structured data to understand entities and relationships, this is a significant missed opportunity. Comprehensive, accurate structured data gives AI models machine-readable confirmation of who you are, what you do, and what you are authoritative about.

    Expecting overnight results. GEO is not a quick fix. It is a sustained strategy that builds over months. I have had clients abandon the process after four weeks because "ChatGPT still does not mention us." Training data cycles take months. Entity authority building takes months. Even RAG-based tools need time to index and surface your content. Set realistic expectations and commit to at least six months of consistent effort.

    Industry-Specific Considerations

    Local service businesses. For plumbers, dentists, lawyers, and other local service providers, AI mentions are heavily influenced by Google Business Profile, local review signals, and local directory presence. I have a dedicated guide on GEO for local businesses that covers this in detail. The short version: optimise your Google Business Profile thoroughly, build consistent local citations, and create content that answers local queries with specific information about your service area.

    SaaS and technology companies. For software companies, product review platforms (G2, Capterra, TrustRadius) are critical. AI tools heavily reference these platforms when recommending software. Having a complete, well-reviewed profile on G2 alone can drive AI mentions for product-category queries. Combine this with comparison content on your blog, developer documentation, and active presence on platforms like Product Hunt and GitHub.

    E-commerce businesses. Product reviews, price comparison data, and detailed product specifications drive AI recommendations for e-commerce. Implement Product schema with offers, reviews, and detailed specifications. Create buying guides and comparison content that AI tools can reference when users ask product recommendation questions.

    Professional services. For consultancies, agencies, and freelance professionals, personal brand authority is paramount. The individual's entity (not just the company entity) needs to be established through thought leadership content, speaking engagements, professional profiles, and published work. My own AI mentions are driven as much by my personal entity authority as by my business entity.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    How long does it take to get mentioned by ChatGPT?

    There is no fixed timeline because it depends on your existing digital footprint, the competitiveness of your industry, and how quickly you build entity authority. In my experience, businesses that already have strong brand mentions across the web and quality content can start appearing in AI responses within two to four months of focused GEO work. For newer brands starting from scratch, expect six to twelve months of consistent effort before you see reliable AI mentions. The key is building a corpus of authoritative, well-structured content that AI models can confidently reference.

    Can I pay to get my business mentioned by ChatGPT?

    No, you cannot directly pay OpenAI or Perplexity to mention your business in organic AI responses. These models generate answers based on their training data and retrieval mechanisms, not advertising placements. However, some AI platforms are beginning to introduce sponsored results -- Perplexity has tested sponsored follow-up questions, for example. The most reliable path to AI mentions remains building genuine authority through quality content, brand mentions on authoritative sites, structured data, and consistent entity information across the web.

    Does blocking AI crawlers hurt my chances of being mentioned?

    Blocking AI crawlers through robots.txt prevents those specific crawlers from accessing your content directly, but it does not guarantee you will not be mentioned. AI models are trained on broad datasets that may already include your content, and retrieval-augmented generation systems like Perplexity use web search results where your content may appear. That said, blocking crawlers reduces the likelihood of fresh content being picked up. I generally recommend allowing AI crawlers for most business sites because the visibility benefit outweighs the risks for non-proprietary content.

    What type of content gets cited most by AI tools?

    AI tools tend to cite content that provides clear, factual, well-structured answers to specific questions. Definitive guides, original research with statistics, comparison articles, how-to content with step-by-step instructions, and FAQ pages perform particularly well. Content that includes specific numbers, named methodologies, unique frameworks, or proprietary data gets cited more frequently because AI models can attribute these to a specific source. Avoid vague marketing copy -- AI tools skip over content that reads like a sales pitch and prefer informational, expertise-driven material.

    Should I optimise for ChatGPT and Perplexity differently?

    Yes, there are differences worth understanding. ChatGPT primarily draws from its training data, so historical content authority and brand recognition matter more. Perplexity uses real-time web search with retrieval-augmented generation, making it more responsive to recently published content and traditional SEO signals. Google AI Overviews pull from Google's index, so standard SEO practices apply most directly there. However, the foundational strategy is the same across all platforms: build entity authority, create citation-worthy content, maintain consistent brand information, and use structured data throughout your site.

    💡
    The bottom line: Getting your business mentioned by ChatGPT, Perplexity, and other AI tools is not about gaming algorithms -- it is about building genuine entity authority across the web. The businesses that get recommended by AI are the ones with strong, consistent brand presence, citation-worthy content, proper structured data, and authority signals from multiple independent sources. Start with your entity foundation, create content that AI tools want to cite, build brand mentions systematically, and monitor your progress.

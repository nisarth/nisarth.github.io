E-commerce Conversion Optimisation: Turning Browsers into Buyers

April 10, 2026 · 15 min read · By Nisarth Patel

Contents

    Getting traffic to an e-commerce website is only half the battle. The other half -- and arguably the more important half -- is converting that traffic into actual purchases. I have worked with online stores that were spending lakhs per month driving visitors to their site but converting less than one percent of them. That is not a traffic problem. That is a conversion optimisation problem, and solving it is usually the fastest path to increasing revenue without spending a single additional rupee on advertising.

    Conversion rate optimisation (CRO) for e-commerce is the systematic process of improving the percentage of website visitors who complete a purchase. It involves understanding user behaviour, identifying friction points in the buying journey, testing hypotheses, and implementing changes that make it easier, faster, and more compelling for visitors to buy. Unlike SEO or paid advertising, which take time to generate results, CRO improvements often deliver immediate revenue increases because they work on your existing traffic.

    Over the past four years, I have helped e-commerce businesses across various categories -- fashion, electronics, home goods, beauty products, and specialty food -- improve their conversion rates. The improvements range from modest (10 to 15 percent) to transformative (50 percent or more), depending on how many issues exist in the current setup. What I have learned is that most e-commerce sites have the same core problems: they are too slow, they do not build enough trust, their checkout process creates unnecessary friction, and they do not recover the inevitable abandoned carts. This guide addresses all of these issues with practical, implementable strategies.

    Site Speed and Its Impact on Conversions

    Site speed is the single most impactful factor in e-commerce conversion optimisation. It is also the most commonly overlooked, perhaps because it feels like a technical problem rather than a marketing one. But the data is unambiguous: slow sites lose sales.

    The cost of every additional second. Research from multiple sources including Google, Deloitte, and Akamai consistently shows that every additional second of page load time reduces e-commerce conversion rates by 7 to 10 percent. For a store generating ten lakh rupees per month in revenue, improving load time from 4 seconds to 2 seconds could add 1.5 to 2 lakh rupees per month in additional revenue -- from the same traffic, without any marketing spend. I ran this exact calculation for a client selling fashion accessories online. Their product pages loaded in 4.7 seconds on mobile. After a focused speed optimisation project that brought load time down to 2.1 seconds, their mobile conversion rate increased by 28 percent within the first month.

    What to optimise first. The highest-impact speed optimisations for e-commerce sites are image compression and format conversion (switching from PNG and JPEG to WebP reduces file sizes by 25 to 35 percent without visible quality loss), lazy loading for images below the fold (especially critical on product listing pages with dozens of product thumbnails), deferring non-critical JavaScript including third-party scripts for analytics, chat widgets, and retargeting pixels, implementing a content delivery network (CDN) to serve static assets from locations closer to your customers, and minimising CSS by removing unused styles that accumulate over time from theme updates and plugin additions.

    Measuring speed correctly. Do not rely solely on lab-based tests like Lighthouse. Field data from real users is what actually matters for conversion. Google's Chrome User Experience Report (CrUX) data, available through PageSpeed Insights and Search Console, shows how real visitors experience your site. I have seen sites with excellent Lighthouse scores but poor field data because the lab test does not account for real-world network conditions, device diversity, and third-party script behaviour. Monitor your Core Web Vitals -- LCP, INP, and CLS -- as both ranking factors and conversion indicators. Set up custom speed monitoring using the Web Vitals JavaScript library to track real user performance continuously.

    Product Photography and Visual Presentation

    In a physical store, customers can touch, try on, and examine products before buying. Online, your product photography must do all of that work. Poor product images are one of the most common reasons shoppers leave without buying, and upgrading photography is one of the most reliable ways to increase conversion rates.

    What good product photography looks like. Every product should have at minimum five to eight images: a clean, white-background hero image showing the full product, close-up detail shots highlighting texture, quality, and key features, scale reference images that show the product in context so shoppers understand the actual size, lifestyle images showing the product being used in a real setting, and for clothing and accessories, images on a model or mannequin from multiple angles. Each image should be high resolution enough to support zoom functionality without pixelation. Shoppers want to inspect products closely, and the ability to zoom in and see fine detail significantly reduces purchase hesitation.

    Video for complex or high-value products. For products above a certain price point or complexity level, video dramatically improves conversion. A 30 to 60 second product video showing the item from all angles, demonstrating how it works, or showing it in use can increase conversion rates by 20 to 40 percent according to multiple studies. I have seen this firsthand with a client selling handcrafted leather bags -- adding 30-second product videos to their top 20 products increased conversion on those pages by 34 percent. The videos do not need to be professionally produced. A well-lit, steady smartphone video showing the product being used is often more authentic and effective than a polished studio production.

    Image optimisation for performance. High-quality images and fast load times are not mutually exclusive. Use responsive images with the srcset attribute to serve appropriately sized images based on the visitor's screen size. Implement WebP format with JPEG fallback for older browsers. Use lazy loading for all images below the initial viewport. For product zoom functionality, load the high-resolution version only when the user initiates a zoom action, not on initial page load. These optimisations let you maintain visual quality while keeping your pages fast.

    Trust Signals That Drive Purchases

    Trust is the invisible currency of e-commerce. Visitors who do not trust your site will not enter their payment details, regardless of how good your products or prices are. Building trust is especially critical for smaller or newer brands that do not have widespread recognition.

    Customer reviews and ratings. Social proof is the most powerful trust signal in e-commerce. Products with reviews convert at significantly higher rates than those without -- studies consistently show a 120 to 270 percent increase in conversion when reviews are present. Implement a robust review system on every product page. Display the average rating prominently near the product title. Show both positive and negative reviews -- a perfect 5-star rating actually looks less trustworthy than a 4.3 to 4.7 rating with a mix of feedback. Include review photos from customers showing the product in real-world use. I actively encourage my e-commerce clients to send post-purchase review request emails five to seven days after delivery, when the customer has had time to use the product but the experience is still fresh.

    Security and payment trust badges. Display SSL security badges, payment provider logos (Visa, Mastercard, UPI, Razorpay, or whatever providers you use), and any relevant security certifications prominently on product pages and throughout the checkout process. These badges reduce anxiety about payment security. Position them near the "Add to Cart" button and again near the payment form during checkout. I ran an A/B test for a client where the only change was adding Razorpay and SSL trust badges below the add-to-cart button. The variant with badges showed a 12 percent increase in add-to-cart rate -- a meaningful improvement from a trivially simple change.

    Clear policies and guarantees. Make your return policy, shipping information, and any satisfaction guarantees clearly visible on product pages, not buried in the footer. A money-back guarantee or free returns policy directly addresses the primary anxiety of online shoppers: "What if I don't like it?" I recommend displaying key policy highlights -- free shipping threshold, return window, satisfaction guarantee -- as icon-based trust elements near the product price. These should be scannable at a glance, not hidden behind links that require additional clicks to read.

    Real business information. Display your business name, physical address, phone number, and email address prominently. Include an "About Us" page with real team photos and your company story. These details signal that a real, accountable business stands behind the website. For smaller brands, this is especially important because shoppers are more cautious when buying from unfamiliar stores. I have seen conversion rate improvements of 8 to 15 percent simply from adding a visible phone number and physical address to the header or footer of an e-commerce site.

    Checkout Optimisation

    The checkout process is where the highest percentage of potential sales is lost. Industry data shows that approximately 70 percent of online shopping carts are abandoned, and a significant portion of that abandonment happens during checkout itself. Optimising your checkout flow is one of the highest-ROI activities in e-commerce.

    Guest checkout is non-negotiable. Requiring account creation before purchase is the single biggest checkout friction point. Research from the Baymard Institute shows that 24 percent of shoppers abandon their cart specifically because the site required them to create an account. Always offer guest checkout as the default option, with account creation presented as an optional step after the purchase is complete. You can incentivise account creation post-purchase by offering order tracking, faster future checkout, or loyalty points -- but never make it a barrier to buying.

    Minimise form fields. Every additional form field increases the likelihood of abandonment. Strip your checkout form to the absolute essentials: name, email, shipping address, and payment information. Do not ask for a phone number unless it is genuinely needed for delivery. Do not separate billing and shipping address by default -- use a "same as shipping" checkbox. Auto-fill the city and state from the postcode. Use a single name field instead of separate first name and last name fields. I have seen checkout completion rates improve by 10 to 18 percent from form field reduction alone.

    Progress indicators and transparency. Show a clear progress bar indicating where the shopper is in the checkout process and how many steps remain. Display an order summary with line items, prices, and totals visible at every step. Never surprise shoppers with additional costs at the last minute -- shipping fees, taxes, and any surcharges should be visible as early as possible in the process, ideally on the cart page before checkout even begins. Unexpected costs at checkout are the number one reason for cart abandonment, cited by 48 percent of abandoners in industry surveys.

    Payment options and flexibility. Offer multiple payment methods including credit and debit cards, UPI, digital wallets (Google Pay, Apple Pay, PhonePe), net banking, and buy-now-pay-later options like Simpl or LazyPay. The more payment options you offer, the fewer customers you lose to payment preference friction. In India, UPI has become the dominant payment method for many demographics, and not supporting it means losing sales unnecessarily. I also recommend implementing one-click payment options for returning customers to reduce friction to the absolute minimum.

    Cart Abandonment Recovery

    Even with a perfectly optimised checkout, a significant percentage of shoppers will abandon their carts. The good news is that a well-designed recovery strategy can recapture 5 to 15 percent of those abandoned carts, representing a substantial revenue recovery.

    Email recovery sequences. An automated abandoned cart email sequence is the most effective recovery tool. The typical sequence consists of three emails. The first email, sent within one hour of abandonment, is a gentle reminder that includes the abandoned product with an image, a direct link back to the cart, and no discount. Many abandonments are simply due to distractions, and a timely reminder is enough to bring the shopper back. The second email, sent 24 hours after abandonment, adds light urgency and may include a small incentive -- free shipping or a 5 to 10 percent discount. The third email, sent 48 to 72 hours after abandonment, creates stronger urgency with messaging about limited stock or expiring discounts. This three-email sequence typically recovers 5 to 10 percent of abandoned carts, with the first email generating the majority of recoveries.

    Exit-intent strategies. Before the shopper even leaves the page, exit-intent technology can detect when the cursor moves toward the browser's close button and trigger a targeted intervention. The most effective exit-intent offers for cart pages are a discount code ("Complete your order now and get 10% off"), free shipping ("You're close! Get free shipping on this order"), or social proof ("432 people bought this product today"). These interventions should be used sparingly -- once per session at most -- and they should be tested to ensure they actually increase net revenue rather than training customers to abandon their carts in order to trigger discounts.

    Retargeting for abandoned carts. Retargeting ads on Facebook, Instagram, and Google Display Network can remind shoppers about products they left behind. Dynamic retargeting that shows the specific products from the abandoned cart is significantly more effective than generic retargeting. Set frequency caps to avoid annoying potential customers -- three to five impressions per day is typically optimal. The retargeting window should match your typical purchase consideration period: one to three days for low-cost impulse products, up to two weeks for higher-consideration purchases.

    WhatsApp and SMS recovery. In India, WhatsApp has become an increasingly effective cart recovery channel. With the user's consent, sending a personalised WhatsApp message with the abandoned product and a direct checkout link can achieve open rates of 90 percent or higher, compared to 40 to 50 percent for email. The key is to keep the message conversational and helpful rather than promotional, and to respect timing -- send during business hours, not late at night. Several platforms now offer automated WhatsApp cart recovery, and the ROI for Indian e-commerce businesses is consistently strong.

    Mobile UX Optimisation

    In India, mobile accounts for 70 to 80 percent of e-commerce traffic and a growing majority of transactions. If your mobile experience is suboptimal, you are losing the majority of your potential customers.

    Thumb-friendly navigation and CTAs. Design your mobile experience around how people actually hold and use their phones. Primary call-to-action buttons should be within the thumb reach zone, which is the lower half of the screen for most users. The "Add to Cart" and "Buy Now" buttons should be large enough to tap without precision -- at least 48 by 48 CSS pixels -- and positioned where they are easy to reach. I recommend a sticky "Add to Cart" bar at the bottom of product pages on mobile that remains visible as the user scrolls through product details, reviews, and related products.

    Simplified mobile navigation. Desktop navigation patterns do not translate to mobile. Your mobile navigation should prioritise the most common user journeys: browsing categories, searching for specific products, accessing the cart, and reaching the account page. Implement a prominent search bar at the top of every page, because mobile users are more likely to search than browse. The hamburger menu should contain a clean, well-organised category structure that does not require more than two taps to reach any product category. Avoid mega-menus on mobile -- they are difficult to navigate on small screens and create frustrating interactions.

    Mobile checkout simplification. Mobile checkout needs to be even more streamlined than desktop. Use large input fields with appropriate keyboard types (numeric keyboard for phone numbers and card numbers, email keyboard for email addresses). Implement autofill compatibility so returning customers can complete forms with a single tap. Offer digital wallet options (Google Pay, Apple Pay) that eliminate form filling entirely. For Indian e-commerce, one-tap UPI payment through apps like Google Pay or PhonePe should be a primary checkout option because it reduces the mobile checkout to a single interaction after the cart page.

    Mobile product page design. On mobile, vertical real estate is precious. Structure your product pages with a clear hierarchy: product image carousel at the top (swipeable, with a visible indicator of how many images are available), product title and price immediately below, followed by key product attributes, a prominent add-to-cart CTA, and then expandable sections for full description, specifications, shipping information, and reviews. Avoid loading all content at once -- use accordion-style expandable sections that keep the page compact while providing access to all information when the shopper wants it.

    Personalisation Strategies

    Personalisation -- showing different content, products, or offers to different visitors based on their behaviour, preferences, or demographics -- is one of the most effective conversion optimisation levers available to e-commerce businesses.

    Behavioural product recommendations. "Customers who bought this also bought" and "Recently viewed" sections are basic personalisation that every e-commerce site should implement. More advanced implementations include personalised homepage content based on browsing history, product recommendations based on purchase history for returning customers, category page sorting based on individual preferences, and personalised search results that prioritise products matching the user's past behaviour. These personalisation features consistently increase average order value by 10 to 30 percent because they help shoppers discover relevant products they might not have found on their own.

    Geo-based personalisation. For Indian e-commerce, showing location-specific information can significantly improve conversion. Display estimated delivery dates based on the visitor's location (determined by IP geolocation). Show prices in the local currency if you serve international customers. Highlight location-specific offers or promotions. Display available payment methods that are popular in the visitor's region. Even something as simple as showing "Delivers to [City] in 2-3 days" on product pages builds confidence and reduces hesitation. I implemented geo-based delivery estimates for a client and saw a 9 percent increase in add-to-cart rate.

    Returning visitor personalisation. Treat returning visitors differently from first-time visitors. Show their recently viewed products prominently. Pre-fill any forms with information from previous visits. Offer loyalty discounts or exclusive access to returning customers. Display personalised messaging that acknowledges their relationship with your brand. The conversion rate for returning visitors is typically two to three times higher than for new visitors, and personalisation amplifies this advantage further.

    Urgency and Scarcity Tactics

    Urgency and scarcity are powerful psychological triggers that can significantly increase conversion rates when used honestly. The operative word is "honestly" -- fake urgency and manufactured scarcity damage trust and can lead to customer complaints and regulatory issues.

    Genuine stock-based scarcity. If a product genuinely has limited stock, displaying "Only 3 left in stock" is a legitimate and effective conversion tactic. This information helps shoppers make informed decisions and creates natural urgency. The key is accuracy -- the stock count must reflect real inventory, not artificial limitations. I have seen "Only 2 left!" messages on sites where the stock refreshes to 2 every few hours, and shoppers notice. This destroys trust far more than it creates urgency. Implement real-time inventory displays connected to your actual stock management system.

    Time-limited offers. Flash sales, limited-time discounts, and countdown timers create urgency when the offer is genuinely time-limited. A 48-hour sale with a visible countdown timer consistently outperforms the same discount without a deadline because the timer creates a concrete reason to buy now rather than later. Use countdown timers on product pages, in email campaigns, and on banner notifications. But never extend a "limited-time" offer indefinitely -- if shoppers learn that your sales never actually end, the urgency effect disappears entirely.

    Social proof as urgency. Real-time social proof creates organic urgency without any manufactured scarcity. Messages like "14 people are viewing this product right now" or "Bought 47 times in the last 24 hours" leverage the fear of missing out through genuine data. Tools like Fomo and ProveSource can display real purchase notifications that show other customers buying products in real time. These notifications are effective because they provide both social proof (other people are buying) and urgency (it might sell out) simultaneously. As with scarcity tactics, the data must be real. Fabricated purchase notifications are not only dishonest but easily detectable and damaging to brand credibility.

    A/B Testing Framework

    Conversion optimisation without A/B testing is just guessing. Every change you make to your site should be validated through testing before being permanently implemented. Here is the framework I use for systematic e-commerce testing.

    Prioritising what to test. Not all tests are equal. I use the ICE framework to prioritise: Impact (how much could this change affect conversion rate?), Confidence (how confident am I that this change will have a positive effect based on data and best practices?), and Ease (how easy is it to implement and run this test?). Each factor is scored from 1 to 10, and the total score determines priority. A change to the checkout flow might score 9 for Impact, 7 for Confidence, and 4 for Ease, giving a total of 20. Adding trust badges might score 5 for Impact, 8 for Confidence, and 9 for Ease, giving a total of 22 and making it the higher-priority test despite lower potential impact, because the confidence and ease make it a better use of resources.

    Running tests correctly. The biggest mistake I see is ending tests too early. You need statistical significance before drawing conclusions, which typically requires at least 200 to 400 conversions per variation for e-commerce tests. Running a test for three days and declaring a winner based on 15 conversions per variant is not testing -- it is coin-flipping. I use a minimum test duration of two full weeks to account for day-of-week variations, and I do not call a test until I have 95 percent statistical significance. Use tools like Google Optimize (or its successors), VWO, or Optimizely that calculate significance automatically.

    What to test on product pages. The highest-impact product page tests I have run include CTA button colour, size, and text (test "Add to Cart" vs "Buy Now" vs "Add to Bag"), product image layout and number of images, price presentation format (testing strikethrough pricing, bundle pricing, EMI breakdowns), review display placement and format, trust badge placement, product description length and format, and the position and design of related product recommendations. Start with the elements closest to the conversion action -- the CTA button and the area immediately surrounding it -- because changes there have the most direct impact on conversion.

    What to test in checkout. Checkout tests include the number of steps versus a single-page checkout, form field order and labels, payment method presentation order, the presence and placement of security badges, shipping option presentation, and the design of order summary. I once ran a test that simply changed the default shipping option from cheapest (5-7 day delivery) to fastest (next-day delivery) with a small surcharge. Despite the higher default cost, overall conversion rate increased by 7 percent because the faster delivery removed a hesitation point for many shoppers, and those who preferred the cheaper option simply changed the selection.

    Analytics Setup for Conversion Tracking

    You cannot optimise what you cannot measure. A proper analytics setup is the foundation of any conversion optimisation programme, yet it is consistently one of the weakest areas I encounter when auditing e-commerce sites.

    Essential e-commerce tracking in GA4. Google Analytics 4 has comprehensive e-commerce tracking capabilities, but they require proper implementation. At minimum, you should be tracking: view_item (product page views), add_to_cart, begin_checkout, add_shipping_info, add_payment_info, and purchase events with full transaction data including revenue, items, and quantities. This creates a complete conversion funnel that shows exactly where visitors drop off. Additionally, track view_item_list on category pages and select_item when shoppers click on a product from a listing. This data reveals which category page layouts and product positions drive the most engagement.

    Enhanced attribution tracking. Set up UTM parameters on every marketing link to understand which channels, campaigns, and content pieces drive the highest-converting traffic. In GA4, use the data-driven attribution model to understand how multiple touchpoints contribute to conversions. Create custom audiences for high-value segments like repeat purchasers, high-AOV customers, and loyal email subscribers. These audiences are valuable both for analysis and for creating targeted remarketing campaigns in Google Ads and Meta Ads.

    Heatmaps and session recordings. Quantitative analytics tells you what is happening but not why. Heatmap tools like Hotjar, Microsoft Clarity, or Crazy Egg show you exactly how users interact with your pages -- where they click, how far they scroll, what they ignore, and where they get confused. Session recordings let you watch real user sessions to identify friction points that numbers alone cannot reveal. I review at least 20 to 30 session recordings per week during active CRO projects, focusing on sessions where users added items to cart but did not complete purchase. These recordings consistently reveal UX issues that I would never have identified through analytics alone.

    Custom dashboards and alerts. Build a conversion optimisation dashboard in Looker Studio that pulls from GA4, your e-commerce platform, and your testing tool. Include conversion rate by device type, traffic source, and landing page category. Set up automated alerts for significant changes -- a sudden drop in mobile conversion rate or a spike in checkout abandonment could indicate a technical issue that needs immediate attention. I set alerts for any daily conversion rate deviation greater than 20 percent from the trailing 7-day average, which has caught broken checkout flows, payment gateway outages, and coupon code errors before they caused significant revenue loss.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    What is a good conversion rate for an e-commerce website?

    The average e-commerce conversion rate across all industries sits between 2 and 3 percent globally. However, what constitutes a good rate varies significantly by industry, traffic source, and product type. Fashion and apparel typically see 1.5 to 2.5 percent, electronics 1 to 2 percent, and health and beauty 3 to 4 percent. Traffic source matters enormously -- email traffic often converts at 4 to 6 percent, organic search at 2 to 3 percent, and social media at 0.5 to 1.5 percent. Rather than benchmarking against industry averages, I recommend focusing on improving your own conversion rate month over month. A store converting at 1.5 percent that improves to 2.25 percent has increased revenue by 50 percent from the same traffic, which is far more meaningful than hitting an arbitrary industry benchmark.

    How much does site speed affect e-commerce conversions?

    Site speed has a dramatic and well-documented impact on e-commerce conversions. Research from Google and various industry studies consistently shows that every additional second of page load time reduces conversion rates by 7 to 10 percent. A site loading in 5 seconds instead of 2 seconds could be losing 20 to 30 percent of potential conversions purely due to speed. Beyond conversions, slow sites also suffer from higher bounce rates, lower average order values, and reduced customer satisfaction. Mobile speed is particularly critical because mobile shoppers are less patient and often on slower connections. I have personally seen conversion rate improvements of 15 to 25 percent from speed optimisation projects that bring load times under 2.5 seconds, making it one of the highest-ROI investments in e-commerce.

    What is the best way to reduce cart abandonment?

    Cart abandonment averages around 70 percent across e-commerce globally, but it can be significantly reduced through a combination of checkout optimisation and recovery strategies. On the prevention side, the most effective tactics are offering guest checkout without requiring account creation, displaying all costs including shipping and taxes before the checkout page, providing multiple payment options including digital wallets and buy-now-pay-later, simplifying the checkout to as few steps as possible, and showing security badges near payment fields. For recovery, an automated email sequence triggered within one hour of abandonment typically recovers 5 to 15 percent of abandoned carts. The first email should be a gentle reminder, the second can include a small incentive, and the third creates urgency with limited availability messaging.

    How do I know which conversion optimisation changes to prioritise?

    Start by analysing your data to identify where the biggest drop-offs occur in your conversion funnel. Use Google Analytics to track the user journey from landing page through product view, add to cart, checkout initiation, and purchase completion. The step with the largest percentage drop-off represents your biggest opportunity. If 60 percent of users who add items to cart never initiate checkout, your cart page or shipping cost presentation is likely the problem. If 40 percent abandon during checkout, your checkout flow needs attention. I also recommend using heatmap tools like Hotjar to understand how users interact with key pages. Prioritise changes using the ICE framework: score each potential change on Impact, Confidence, and Ease of implementation. Start with high-impact, high-confidence, easy-to-implement changes first.

    Should I use pop-ups on my e-commerce site?

    Pop-ups can be effective for e-commerce when used thoughtfully, but they can also damage conversions and user experience when implemented poorly. Exit-intent pop-ups offering a first-purchase discount typically convert at 2 to 5 percent and can recover visitors who would otherwise leave without purchasing. Timed pop-ups that appear after 30 to 60 seconds of browsing can be effective for email capture. However, aggressive pop-ups that appear immediately on page load, cover the entire screen on mobile, or are difficult to dismiss will hurt your conversion rate, increase bounce rate, and may trigger Google's intrusive interstitial penalty. My recommendation is to limit pop-ups to one per session, use exit-intent triggers rather than time-based triggers for discount offers, ensure they are easily dismissible on both desktop and mobile, and always A/B test pop-up versus no pop-up to verify they are actually improving your bottom line.

    💡
    The bottom line: Conversion optimisation is the fastest path to e-commerce revenue growth because it makes your existing traffic more valuable. Start with site speed, build trust through reviews and clear policies, optimise your checkout flow to remove friction, recover abandoned carts systematically, and test every significant change before implementing it permanently. Even small improvements compound dramatically over time -- a 0.5 percent conversion rate increase on a site with 50,000 monthly visitors and a 2,000 rupee average order value adds over 5 lakh rupees in annual revenue.

Zero-Click Searches: How to Win When Users Never Click

April 10, 2026 · 14 min read · By Nisarth Patel

Contents

    Here is a reality that still catches many business owners off guard: the majority of Google searches now end without a single click to any website. The user types their query, gets the answer directly on the search results page, and moves on. No visit to your site. No chance to convert them with your carefully crafted landing page. No analytics event to track. This phenomenon is called a zero-click search, and it has fundamentally changed how we need to think about search engine optimisation. If your entire SEO strategy is built around driving clicks, you are optimising for a shrinking slice of the pie.

    I have been working with businesses in Ahmedabad and across India who come to me worried about declining organic traffic despite stable or even improving rankings. When we dig into the data, the pattern is almost always the same -- their target keywords are increasingly being answered directly on the SERP through featured snippets, knowledge panels, local packs, and AI Overviews. The traffic has not disappeared because they lost rankings. It has disappeared because Google is keeping users on its own page.

    This guide is about adapting to that reality. I will walk you through what zero-click searches are, why they are growing, and most importantly, how to turn SERP visibility into real business value even when users never click through to your website. This is not theoretical speculation -- it is built from strategies I use with clients every day.

    What Are Zero-Click Searches

    A zero-click search occurs when a user performs a Google search and gets the information they need directly from the search engine results page without clicking on any organic or paid result. Google provides the answer through one of its many SERP features, and the user's query is satisfied without ever leaving Google.

    The term was popularised by Rand Fishkin and the research team at SparkToro, who analysed clickstream data and found that a significant majority of Google searches resulted in no click to any external website. Their research has been updated multiple times, and the trend has only accelerated. By 2026, estimates suggest that between 60 and 65 percent of all desktop searches and over 70 percent of mobile searches are zero-click.

    It is important to understand that zero-click searches are not all the same. Some are genuinely useful to users -- if someone searches for the current time in New York, showing the answer instantly is a better experience than making them click through to a website. Others are more controversial, particularly when Google extracts content from a website and displays it so completely that there is no reason for the user to visit the source. The ethical debate aside, the practical reality for businesses is that you need a strategy for both scenarios.

    There are several types of SERP features that create zero-click results. Featured snippets pull a direct answer from a webpage and display it prominently at the top of results. Knowledge panels show structured information about entities like businesses, people, and places. Local packs display a map with three business listings for local queries. People Also Ask (PAA) boxes provide expandable answers to related questions. AI Overviews generate synthesised answers using information from multiple sources. Direct answer boxes show factual information like calculations, weather, and conversions. Each of these presents different opportunities and requires different optimisation approaches.

    The Data Behind Zero-Click Growth

    Understanding the scale of zero-click searches helps you appreciate why adapting your strategy is not optional -- it is urgent. The data tells a compelling story about how search behaviour has shifted over the past several years.

    In 2019, SparkToro's analysis of clickstream data showed that approximately 50 percent of Google searches ended without a click. That number has grown consistently each year as Google has expanded its SERP features and introduced AI Overviews. The introduction of AI Overviews in particular has accelerated the trend because these synthesised answers are often comprehensive enough to satisfy the user's query entirely.

    Mobile searches have always had a higher zero-click rate than desktop, and this gap continues to widen. On mobile devices, SERP features take up a larger proportion of the visible screen, and users are more inclined to accept a quick answer rather than load a full webpage on a potentially slow connection. For local searches on mobile, the zero-click rate is particularly high because the local pack provides phone numbers, directions, hours, and reviews -- everything a user typically needs to make a decision.

    What is particularly interesting is the distribution across query types. Navigational queries, where users search for a specific brand or website, have always had high click-through rates because the user has a clear destination in mind. Informational queries -- the "what is," "how to," and "why does" questions -- are where zero-click rates are highest. Transactional queries, where users are looking to buy something, tend to maintain reasonable click-through rates because users need to visit a site to complete a purchase. This distribution matters because it should inform where you focus your optimisation efforts.

    I track zero-click trends for my clients by comparing impressions to clicks in Google Search Console. When I see a keyword with thousands of impressions but a click-through rate below two percent, that is almost certainly a zero-click query. Rather than abandoning those keywords, I shift the strategy to maximise the value we extract from that visibility.

    Brand Visibility in a Zero-Click World

    The biggest mindset shift required by zero-click searches is recognising that visibility has value even without clicks. Traditional SEO metrics focus heavily on clicks and traffic. But in a zero-click world, impressions matter too -- not in the vague, hand-wavy sense that some marketers use to justify poor results, but in concrete, measurable ways.

    When your brand name appears in a featured snippet, knowledge panel, or local pack, users see it. They may not click through today, but that exposure builds brand recognition. Research on the mere exposure effect in psychology shows that people develop a preference for things they are familiar with, simply through repeated exposure. Every time a user sees your brand in search results, even without clicking, you are building a mental association between your brand and the topic they searched for.

    I have measured this effect with clients by tracking branded search volume over time. When we successfully win featured snippets and SERP features for non-branded informational queries, we consistently see an increase in branded searches in the following weeks and months. People who see your brand answering their questions start searching for your brand directly when they are ready to take action. This is particularly powerful for service businesses and consultants because it builds authority and trust before the prospect ever visits your website.

    There is also the competitive angle. If a featured snippet for a key query in your industry is showing your competitor's content, that is your competitor getting the brand exposure instead of you. Even if neither of you gets a click, the business whose name appears is winning the awareness battle. In many of the industries I work with -- from digital marketing to local services in Gujarat -- the competition for SERP features is still relatively low. Many businesses have not adapted their strategy at all, which means there is a window of opportunity for those who move quickly.

    The practical implication is that your SEO reporting needs to include SERP feature tracking alongside traditional click and traffic metrics. Tools like Semrush and Ahrefs track featured snippet ownership and SERP feature appearances. Google Search Console shows impressions and click-through rates at the query level. By monitoring these metrics together, you get a much more complete picture of your search visibility.

    Winning Featured Snippets

    Featured snippets are the most prominent zero-click feature and the one where you have the most direct control over whether your content appears. A featured snippet is a block of content pulled from a webpage and displayed at the top of search results, above the first organic listing. It can be a paragraph, a list, a table, or occasionally a video.

    The first step in winning featured snippets is identifying which of your target keywords trigger them. Search for your target queries and note which ones display a featured snippet. Tools like Semrush and Ahrefs can filter your keyword list to show only those with a featured snippet in the SERP. Once you have this list, prioritise keywords where you already rank in the top ten because Google overwhelmingly pulls featured snippets from pages that are already on page one.

    The structure of your content matters enormously for featured snippet eligibility. For paragraph snippets, which are the most common type, you need to provide a concise, direct answer to the query within the first 40 to 60 words of the relevant section. I use a pattern I call "question-answer-elaboration" -- start with the question as a heading, immediately provide a direct answer in the first sentence or two, then expand with additional context and detail. Google wants to extract a clean, self-contained answer, so make it easy.

    For list-based featured snippets, use properly formatted HTML lists -- ordered lists for step-by-step processes and unordered lists for non-sequential items. If Google detects a clear list structure on your page, it is more likely to pull it as a list snippet. I have seen pages jump into the featured snippet position simply by reformatting prose paragraphs into structured lists.

    Table snippets are particularly powerful for comparison content. If you are creating content that compares features, prices, specifications, or any other data that naturally fits into rows and columns, use an actual HTML table rather than describing the data in paragraphs. Google's ability to extract and display table data has improved significantly, and table snippets stand out visually in the SERP.

    One thing I always tell clients is that winning a featured snippet is not a one-time achievement. Google frequently rotates the source of featured snippets, especially for competitive queries. You need to monitor your snippet ownership and be prepared to update and improve your content if you lose a snippet. I check featured snippet status weekly for high-priority keywords and have a process for rapid content updates when we lose one. The featured snippets guide I wrote goes into much more detail on specific optimisation techniques.

    Knowledge Panels and Entity Building

    Knowledge panels are the information boxes that appear on the right side of desktop search results (or at the top on mobile) when Google has confident information about an entity -- a person, business, organisation, place, or thing. Unlike featured snippets, you cannot directly optimise your content to appear in a knowledge panel. Instead, you need to build your entity's presence across the web so that Google's Knowledge Graph includes your information.

    The foundation of knowledge panel optimisation is establishing your entity across authoritative sources. For a business, this starts with your Google Business Profile, which is the single most important factor for triggering a knowledge panel for brand searches. Make sure your profile is complete with accurate business name, category, address, phone number, website, hours, photos, and a detailed description. I wrote a comprehensive Google Business Profile optimisation guide that covers this in detail.

    Beyond Google Business Profile, you want to establish your entity on Wikipedia (if notable enough), Wikidata, Crunchbase, LinkedIn, and relevant industry directories. The key is consistency -- your business name, address, and core information should be identical across all these sources. Discrepancies confuse Google's entity resolution algorithms and reduce your chances of earning a knowledge panel.

    For personal brands and consultants, which is relevant for many of my clients, the approach is similar but focuses on different platforms. A well-structured personal website with clear About page, published articles or media appearances, social media profiles, and any relevant professional directory listings all contribute to your entity profile. I recommend including structured data -- specifically Person schema with sameAs properties linking to your profiles -- on your website to explicitly connect your web presence.

    Once you have a knowledge panel, you can claim it through Google's verification process and suggest edits to ensure the information is accurate. This is a step many businesses overlook. An unclaimed knowledge panel may display outdated or incorrect information, and you have no control over it until you go through the verification process. Claimed panels allow you to suggest changes to the featured image, description, and social profiles displayed.

    The value of a knowledge panel in the zero-click context is significant. When someone searches for your brand name, the knowledge panel provides your phone number, website, hours, location, and often reviews and social links -- all without the user needing to click through to your site. This is not traffic you are losing; it is information you are delivering more efficiently. The user may still visit your site for deeper engagement, but the knowledge panel handles the initial information needs instantly.

    Dominating the Local Pack

    For local businesses, the local pack -- the map with three business listings that appears for local-intent queries -- is arguably the most important zero-click feature. When someone searches for "plumber in Ahmedabad" or "best restaurant near me," the local pack is what they see first. And many users will call a business, get directions, or read reviews directly from the local pack without ever clicking through to the business website.

    The local pack is powered primarily by Google Business Profile data, which means your GBP optimisation is directly tied to your zero-click search performance. The three main ranking factors for local pack inclusion are relevance (how well your business matches the search query), distance (how close your business is to the searcher), and prominence (how well-known and well-reviewed your business is).

    Relevance is influenced by your primary and secondary business categories, your business description, your services, and the content on your website. Choose your primary category carefully -- it should be the most specific category that accurately describes your business. If you are a "digital marketing consultant," use that rather than the broader "marketing agency" category. Add all relevant secondary categories as well, but do not add categories that do not genuinely apply to your business.

    Reviews are the prominence factor you have the most control over, and they matter enormously for local pack rankings. Businesses with more reviews and higher average ratings consistently appear higher in local pack results. I help my clients implement systematic review generation strategies -- following up with satisfied customers at the right time, making it easy to leave a review, and responding to every review (positive and negative) to show engagement. The volume and recency of reviews both matter, so this needs to be an ongoing effort, not a one-time push.

    One aspect of local pack optimisation that many businesses miss is Google Business Profile posts. These are short updates that appear on your GBP listing and can include promotions, events, product updates, or general content. Regular posting signals to Google that your listing is actively managed, and the posts themselves can provide additional information that influences which queries your listing appears for. I recommend posting at least once per week with content relevant to your services.

    For the zero-click dimension specifically, make sure your GBP listing contains all the information a potential customer might need without visiting your website. This includes complete business hours (including special hours for holidays), a list of services with descriptions and prices where applicable, high-quality photos of your business, products, and team, and a direct phone number. The more complete your listing, the more likely users are to take action directly from the SERP, which is a win even though it does not register as website traffic.

    People Also Ask Optimisation

    The People Also Ask (PAA) box is one of the most underutilised zero-click opportunities I see. PAA boxes appear in the majority of search results and contain expandable questions related to the original query. When a user clicks on a question, an answer is pulled from a webpage, and additional related questions are dynamically loaded. This creates an expanding tree of questions that can drive significant visibility.

    What makes PAA particularly interesting from a zero-click perspective is that each expanded answer includes a link to the source page. While many users get their answer from the expanded text alone, the click-through rate from PAA boxes is actually higher than you might expect -- users who actively expand a question are more engaged than those passively reading a featured snippet. Research suggests PAA click-through rates can be between 3 and 8 percent, which may sound low but represents meaningful traffic for high-volume queries.

    To optimise for PAA, you need to structure your content around the questions your target audience is asking. I use several tools to identify PAA questions: searching for my target keywords and noting the questions that appear, using AlsoAsked.com which maps the full tree of PAA questions for any query, and analysing Google's autocomplete suggestions. Once I have a list of relevant questions, I create content that directly answers them.

    The format matters. Google tends to pull PAA answers from content that uses the question as a heading (H2 or H3) and follows it immediately with a concise answer of 40 to 60 words. This is similar to featured snippet optimisation, and in fact, the same content can appear in both. I structure FAQ sections specifically for PAA eligibility -- each question as a heading, followed by a direct answer paragraph, then expanded detail. Implementing Answer Engine Optimisation (AEO) principles is essential for maximising PAA visibility.

    One advanced technique I use is creating "question hub" pages that target clusters of related PAA questions. Instead of scattering answers across multiple blog posts, I create comprehensive guides that address ten to fifteen related questions in one place. These pages tend to accumulate PAA appearances across multiple queries because Google recognises them as thorough resources on a topic. The key is maintaining quality -- each answer needs to be genuinely helpful, not just keyword-stuffed to game the system.

    AI Overviews and the Future of Zero-Click

    AI Overviews represent the next evolution of zero-click search, and they are changing the landscape faster than any previous SERP feature. When Google generates an AI Overview for a query, it synthesises information from multiple sources into a comprehensive answer that appears at the very top of the results page. These overviews are often detailed enough to satisfy complex queries that previously would have required clicking through to multiple websites.

    The impact on click-through rates has been significant. Early data from various studies suggests that AI Overviews reduce organic click-through rates by 20 to 40 percent for queries where they appear. That is a substantial drop, and it is why many in the SEO industry have been concerned about the direction Google is heading. However, there are nuances that the headline numbers miss.

    First, AI Overviews include source citations -- links to the websites whose content was used to generate the overview. Being cited as a source in an AI Overview is a new form of visibility that carries significant trust signals. When Google's AI explicitly references your content as a source, it validates your authority on the topic. I have seen client sites experience traffic increases from AI Overview citations because a smaller percentage of users click through, but the total volume of searches triggering the overview is large enough to generate meaningful traffic.

    Second, AI Overviews tend to appear for informational queries rather than transactional ones. If someone is researching a topic, the AI Overview may satisfy their need. But if they are looking to buy a product, hire a service provider, or take a specific action, they still need to visit a website. This means the impact is disproportionately felt by content-focused publishers rather than service or e-commerce businesses.

    To position your content for AI Overview citations, focus on creating authoritative, well-structured content that clearly states facts, provides unique data or perspectives, and uses proper schema markup. The principles of Generative Engine Optimisation (GEO) apply directly here -- you are optimising not just for traditional search algorithms but for the AI systems that generate these overviews. I recommend reading my detailed guide on GEO for the full framework.

    Looking further ahead, the trend toward zero-click is only going to accelerate. As AI models become more capable and search engines integrate more generative features, an increasing proportion of queries will be answered without clicks. The businesses that thrive will be those that adapt their strategies to extract value from visibility itself, not just from website visits.

    Measuring Zero-Click Impact

    One of the biggest challenges with zero-click searches is measurement. Traditional analytics tools are built around tracking visits, pageviews, and on-site behaviour. They have no way to measure the value of an impression that never results in a visit. But you can build a measurement framework that captures most of the impact with the tools already available to you.

    Start with Google Search Console, which is your primary data source for zero-click analysis. For each query your site appears for, Search Console shows impressions, clicks, click-through rate, and average position. Sort your queries by impressions in descending order, then look at the click-through rate column. Queries with high impressions but very low CTR (below 2 percent) are your zero-click queries. These are searches where your content is being seen but not clicked, likely because a SERP feature is satisfying the query directly.

    Next, segment your queries by type. I categorise them into three buckets: branded queries (searches that include your business name), informational queries (questions and research-intent searches), and transactional queries (searches with purchase or action intent). The expected CTR differs dramatically between these categories. A low CTR on a branded query is a problem. A low CTR on an informational query where you own the featured snippet is expected and potentially fine.

    Track your branded search volume over time as an indicator of zero-click brand-building effects. If your unbranded visibility is increasing (more impressions for informational queries), you should see a corresponding increase in branded searches as users who encounter your brand through SERP features start searching for you directly. I track this monthly for all my clients and it is one of the strongest correlations I have observed.

    For local businesses, track phone calls, direction requests, and website clicks from your Google Business Profile dashboard. These are actions taken directly from the local pack and knowledge panel -- the definition of zero-click engagement. If your GBP is generating thirty phone calls per week, that is thirty leads that never showed up in your website analytics but came directly from your search presence.

    Third-party tools add additional visibility. Semrush's Position Tracking feature tracks which SERP features your site appears in for each tracked keyword. Ahrefs' SERP Features report shows your featured snippet ownership over time. These tools let you set up automated alerts when you gain or lose a SERP feature, so you can respond quickly. I also use Google Alerts and Brand24 for brand mention monitoring, which captures instances where your brand is referenced in contexts that may have originated from zero-click visibility.

    Turning Visibility into Conversions

    Visibility without conversion is just vanity. The real question is: how do you turn zero-click impressions into actual business results? This is where strategy becomes essential, because the conversion path in a zero-click world is fundamentally different from the traditional "rank, get click, convert on landing page" model.

    The first conversion mechanism is branded search. As I mentioned earlier, consistent SERP feature visibility drives branded search volume. When users see your brand repeatedly answering their questions, they develop trust and recognition. When they are ready to take action -- hire a consultant, buy a product, engage a service -- they search for your brand directly. This branded traffic converts at significantly higher rates than non-branded organic traffic because the user already has familiarity and trust. For my own practice, I have tracked a direct correlation between featured snippet wins for competitive informational keywords and increases in direct inquiries through my contact page.

    The second mechanism is optimising the SERP features themselves for conversion signals. Your featured snippet content, knowledge panel information, and local pack listing are all touchpoints where you can influence user behaviour. In your featured snippet content, include your brand name naturally so that users associate the answer with your business. In your knowledge panel, make sure your phone number and website are prominent and accurate. In your local pack listing, an updated description, quality photos, and strong reviews all influence whether a user chooses your business over a competitor.

    The third mechanism is creating click-worthy content for the subset of users who do want more information. Even in zero-click scenarios, some users will click through. Your meta title and description should make a compelling case for why visiting your page offers more value than the SERP feature alone. Use phrases like "with examples," "step-by-step guide," or "real case study" to signal that your page contains depth beyond the snippet answer. I have improved click-through rates on zero-click queries by 30 to 50 percent just by rewriting meta descriptions to emphasise the additional value on the page.

    The fourth mechanism is retargeting. If a user does click through from a SERP feature, even briefly, you can add them to a retargeting audience. This lets you continue the conversation through display ads or social media, converting a brief visit into an ongoing touchpoint. For clients with retargeting budgets, I set up specific audiences for visitors who arrive from featured snippet clicks, as these users have already demonstrated interest in the topic and familiarity with the brand.

    Finally, consider how your content strategy can guide users from zero-click awareness to high-intent engagement. If you rank in featured snippets for broad informational queries, create clear pathways from that content to your service pages, case studies, and contact forms. Internal linking from your informational content to your transactional pages ensures that users who do click through can easily move toward conversion.

    Building a Zero-Click Strategy Step by Step

    Let me walk you through the process I use with clients to build a comprehensive zero-click search strategy from scratch. This is not a one-time setup -- it is an ongoing programme that evolves as the SERP landscape changes.

    Step one: audit your current SERP feature landscape. Export your top 100 to 200 keywords from Google Search Console or your rank tracking tool. For each keyword, check which SERP features currently appear. Note whether your site appears in any of those features and which competitors own the features you do not. This gives you a map of opportunities and threats.

    Step two: categorise and prioritise. Group your keywords by the type of SERP feature they trigger. Featured snippet keywords go in one bucket, local pack keywords in another, PAA keywords in a third. Within each bucket, prioritise by search volume, business relevance, and current ranking position. You have the best chance of winning SERP features for keywords where you already rank in the top five to ten positions.

    Step three: optimise existing content. Before creating anything new, update your existing content to target SERP features. Add concise answer paragraphs below relevant headings. Restructure content into lists and tables where appropriate. Add FAQ sections with clear question-answer pairs. Implement schema markup -- FAQPage schema, HowTo schema, and any other relevant types. These changes can often win SERP features within days to weeks.

    Step four: create feature-targeted content. For SERP features that none of your existing content can win, create new content specifically designed to target them. Question hub pages for PAA clusters, comparison tables for table snippets, local landing pages for local pack visibility. Every piece of new content should have a clear SERP feature target in addition to its traditional SEO goals.

    Step five: optimise your entity presence. Ensure your Google Business Profile is complete and actively managed. Claim your knowledge panel if one exists. Build consistent citations across relevant directories. Implement Organisation, Person, and LocalBusiness schema on your website. These entity signals influence knowledge panels, local packs, and AI Overview citations.

    Step six: measure and iterate. Set up tracking for SERP feature ownership, branded search volume, GBP actions, and traditional click metrics. Review the data monthly and adjust your strategy based on what is working. When you lose a SERP feature, investigate why and update your content. When you win one, study what worked and apply those patterns to other keywords.

    This is the approach I take with every client, and it consistently delivers results. The businesses that commit to a zero-click strategy see improvements not just in SERP feature ownership but in overall brand awareness, branded search volume, and ultimately, conversions. The search landscape has changed, and the strategies that worked five years ago are no longer sufficient. But the businesses that adapt have an enormous advantage over those that do not.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    What percentage of Google searches are zero-click?

    Current research indicates that approximately 60 to 65 percent of Google searches result in zero clicks, meaning the user finds their answer directly on the search results page without clicking through to any website. This figure has been rising steadily since 2019 and is expected to continue growing as Google expands AI Overviews, featured snippets, and knowledge panels. On mobile devices, the zero-click rate is even higher, often exceeding 70 percent, because the SERP features take up more screen space and users are more likely to accept quick answers. The trend varies by industry and query type, but the overall direction is clear and accelerating.

    Are zero-click searches bad for my website traffic?

    Not necessarily. While zero-click searches do reduce the number of clicks to your website for certain queries, they also create brand visibility opportunities that did not exist before. When your business name, phone number, or expertise appears in a featured snippet, knowledge panel, or local pack, users see your brand even without clicking. This builds recognition and trust over time. The key is to shift your strategy to account for both click-based and impression-based value. Focus on winning SERP features for awareness queries while optimising deeper content for transactional queries where users are more likely to click through and convert on your website.

    How do I track zero-click search performance?

    Google Search Console is your primary tool. Compare impressions against clicks to calculate your click-through rate for individual queries. A high-impression, low-click query is likely generating zero-click results. Use the Search Appearance filter to identify which SERP features your pages appear in. Third-party tools like Semrush and Ahrefs track featured snippet ownership and SERP feature changes over time. For brand monitoring, set up Google Alerts and use tools like Brand24 to track mentions that may stem from zero-click visibility. The goal is to measure both direct clicks and the indirect impact on branded search volume and conversions.

    Which types of queries are most likely to be zero-click?

    Informational queries with simple, factual answers are the most common zero-click searches. Questions like "what is the capital of France" or "how tall is Mount Everest" are answered directly in the SERP. Local queries such as "restaurants near me" or "plumber in Ahmedabad" often result in zero clicks to websites because users interact with the local pack directly -- calling, getting directions, or reading reviews without visiting the business website. Weather, calculator, unit conversion, and sports score queries are almost entirely zero-click. Navigational queries where users search for a brand name also tend to have low click-through because the knowledge panel provides the essential information upfront.

    Should I stop trying to rank for zero-click keywords?

    Absolutely not. Ranking for zero-click keywords still provides significant value through brand visibility and authority building. When your content is the source behind a featured snippet, users associate your brand with expertise on that topic. This creates downstream effects -- increased branded searches, higher trust when users encounter your brand later, and more referral traffic from people who do click through for deeper information. The strategy should not be to avoid zero-click keywords but to optimise specifically for the SERP features that appear for those queries. Win the featured snippet, dominate the People Also Ask section, and ensure your local pack listing is complete and compelling.

    💡
    The bottom line: Zero-click searches are not a threat to be feared -- they are a shift to be leveraged. The businesses that win in this new landscape are those that optimise for visibility across all SERP features, build strong entity presence, and create conversion pathways that work even when users never visit their website. Start by auditing your current SERP feature landscape, then systematically optimise for featured snippets, knowledge panels, local packs, and PAA boxes. If you want help building a zero-click strategy tailored to your business, get in touch and I will walk you through it.

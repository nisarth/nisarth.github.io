What Is GEO and Why Businesses Can't Ignore It in 2026

NP
Nisarth Patel
January 15, 2026 · 7 min read

Something changed in how people search for information — and most businesses haven't caught up with it yet. When someone types a question into ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Google's AI Overview, they don't get ten blue links. They get an answer. And that answer either includes your business, or it doesn't.

GEO — Generative Engine Optimisation — is the practice of making sure your business is the one that shows up in those AI-generated answers. It's not SEO for chatbots. It's a different discipline, built on different signals, using different techniques. The overlap with traditional SEO is real but limited.

Why this matters right now

AI search tools saw a dramatic increase in usage through 2024. ChatGPT's search function, Perplexity's growing user base, Google's AI Overviews rolling out in India — these aren't experiments any more. A meaningful share of search intent is shifting toward conversational AI. For some industries and query types, it's already the primary channel.

The businesses that get mentioned by AI tools aren't always the biggest ones. They're often the ones with the clearest, most citable content. That's the opportunity.

How AI tools decide what to cite

AI search tools pull from a mix of sources — their training data, indexed web pages, and live retrieval (in the case of Perplexity and similar tools). When an AI is generating an answer, it looks for sources that are:

  • Clearly written and directly answer the question
  • Structured well — headings, concise paragraphs, defined entities
  • Authoritative — well-cited, linked from other credible sources
  • Current — recently published or updated

This overlaps with what makes good SEO content. But there are differences. A featured snippet position doesn't guarantee AI citation. And some content that ranks well on traditional search does poorly in AI-generated answers because it's structured for human skimming, not machine parsing.

What GEO actually involves

There's no definitive playbook yet — the field is less than two years old in its current form. But based on what I've seen working across multiple clients, the effective approaches include:

1. Clear entity definition

AI tools think in entities — people, businesses, locations, products, concepts. The clearer your website is about what entity it represents, the better. This means structured About pages with unambiguous information, consistent NAP (Name, Address, Phone) across the web, and schema markup that defines your business type, location, and offers.

2. Direct-answer content

Write content that directly answers the questions your customers are asking. Not indirectly. Not in a buried paragraph five sections down. Lead with the answer, then explain it. AI tools prefer content that states the answer in the first sentence or two, then provides context below.

3. Citation-worthy sourcing

AI tools trust content that other trusted sources trust. Get mentioned in industry publications, local news, directories relevant to your field. A mention in a trusted source carries more weight for AI citation than it does for traditional SEO, because AI tools are actively trying to identify which sources are authoritative on a given topic.

4. Consistent content velocity

Publishing regularly signals that your site is active and being maintained. For tools with live retrieval, recent content is weighted more heavily. A blog that publishes every few weeks is more likely to be current in AI responses than a site that hasn't been updated in six months.

What GEO is not

It's not keyword stuffing for chatbots. It's not a technical trick. And it's not something you can fully control — AI tools make their own decisions about what to cite based on their own models. What you can control is giving them more reasons to choose you over a competitor.

Where to start

If you're just getting into this, the most practical starting point is to audit what your site currently says versus what your customers actually ask. Find the ten most common questions your customers have about your business or category. Then check whether your website answers any of them clearly and directly. Chances are, it doesn't — and that's the first gap to close.

After that, look at your structured data, entity definition, and off-site mentions. That gives you a clear picture of where you stand.

💡
The short version: GEO is about making your content clear, citable, and authoritative enough that AI tools choose to include it in their answers. The technical and creative work are different from traditional SEO, but the underlying goal is the same — get in front of people when they're looking for what you offer.
